# 王慧文产品调研方法论 Skill

A structured research framework distilled from Wang Huiwen's Tsinghua product management course. Apply these lenses in sequence, weaving findings together into a strategic narrative.

---

## Phase 0 — Define Scope

Before running any analysis, clarify:
- **What market / product / opportunity** is being researched?
- **Whose perspective**: investor, founder, PM, incumbent?
- **Time horizon**: near-term (1–2 yr) or long-term structural?

---

## Phase 1 — TAM & Market Sizing (市场体量)

> "If you can only look at one factor, look at market size."

**Steps:**
1. **Current state**: What is the known market size (orders, revenue, users)?  
2. **Growth rate fitting**: Use current volume × growth rate to project 3–5 yr trajectory.  
3. **First-principles sizing**: Trace back to fundamental human needs (马斯洛需求), social trends, and urbanization. Ask: which self-sufficient activities are being outsourced to social division of labor?  
4. **Analogy method (类比法)**: Find a comparable mature market in another country/era and use it as a ceiling estimate.  
5. **Calibration check**: Has the market been mis-sized historically (too large or too small)? Why?

**Output**: Best estimate of TAM, with 3 scenarios (conservative / base / optimistic). Note key assumptions that would change the estimate.

---

## Phase 2 — Scale Effects (规模效应)

> "Scale effects are the gravity of the business world."

Determine the **shape** of the scale-effect curve:

| Curve | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **A (Exponential)** | Network effects — value ∝ nodes² | WeChat, telephone networks |
| **B (Linear)** | Each new user adds equal value | Taobao (marketplace) |
| **C (Logarithmic)** | Dual-sided with same-side negative effects | Ride-hailing, delivery |

**Then determine Scope:**
- **Global** scale effects (WhatsApp)
- **National** scale effects (Taobao)
- **City-level** scale effects (group-buy, ride-hailing)
- **Hyperlocal / honeycomb** (food delivery — each cell fights independently)

**Then assess Parameters:**
- Even within the same curve type, the steepness/coefficient varies enormously. Compare real-user behavior across cohorts at different scale levels.

**Critical Mass**: For A-curve businesses, identify the inflection point. First past critical mass typically dominates.

---

## Phase 3 — Matthew Effect & Market Concentration (马太效应 & 市场集中度)

> "Most decision-makers lack the information to judge independently — so they follow signals."

1. **Predict end-state concentration**: Will this market end with 1, 2–3, 7, or many players?  
   - 1 player → exponential scale effects (social networks)  
   - 2–3 players → fierce competition; get into top-3 fast  
   - Many players → differentiation is the winning strategy  

2. **CR3 today**: What are the top-3 players' combined share?  

3. **Matthew flywheel**: How does leading signal (brand, investment quality, talent) compound? Map the flywheel explicitly.  

4. **Implication for entry strategy**: If market ends at 2–3, ROI-optimizing strategy is to be in top-3 with highest ROI, not necessarily largest volume.

---

## Phase 4 — Industry Chain & Value Distribution (产业链)

> "Every industry chain has a 'chain master' — identify who holds structural power."

1. Map the full value chain from raw supply to end consumer.  
2. Identify the **chain master (链主)** — who sets standards, captures margin, and leads during disruption?  
3. Apply the **Smile Curve**: value concentrates at upstream (core IP / supply) and downstream (consumer interface). Middle manufacturing/execution is squeezed.  
4. Identify **ecosystem plays**: Is there a platform that bundles multiple chain roles (like Amazon in publishing)? Could the target business become one?  
5. **Porter's Five Forces** lens: Focus especially on supplier concentration vs. buyer concentration. Best position = fragmented suppliers, fragmented buyers, concentrated self.

---

## Phase 5 — First-Mover vs. Late-Mover (先发与后发)

> "Late movers face smaller cognitive blind spots — innovators are often trapped by their own creative constraints."

| First-mover advantages | Late-mover advantages |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| Innovation brand label | No need to educate market |
| Earlier talent & resource accumulation | Proven demand; less uncertainty |
| Proprietary learning (pit knowledge) | Normal commercial logic applies |
| | Can target markets pioneer ignored |

**Key question**: What blind spots does the pioneer have? (e.g., Ele.me ignored non-campus markets; early social nets ignored real-name identity.)

---

## Phase 6 — Incremental vs. Existing Market (增量与存量)

> "In existing (存量) markets, essentially the entire world is your enemy."

1. What is the current **penetration rate**? If >50%, market is largely 存量.  
2. **Customer acquisition cost gap**: Incremental market CAC is typically 10× lower than poaching from incumbents.  
3. Even in 存量 markets, opportunity exists through **differentiation** targeting new cohorts (Pepsi's "Next Generation" play).

---

## Phase 7 — Frequency Analysis (高频低频)

> "High-frequency apps beat low-frequency apps — but low-frequency can still win if you go deep in the value chain."

1. What is the natural usage frequency of this product/service?  
2. If low-frequency, strategies to survive:  
   - **Go deep in the industry chain** (Lianjia / Beike: offline agents, financial services, housing data)  
   - **Partner with high-frequency platforms** (Lianjia inside WeChat; Meituan inside WeChat)  
3. High-frequency advantage compounds through app placement, memory, startup speed, and cross-sell.

---

## Phase 8 — Market Timing (入场时机)

> "Timing windows are like lightning — they open and close in weeks, not years."

Apply the **PEST** lens to explain *why now*:
- **Political**: regulatory changes opening/closing opportunity  
- **Economic**: cost curves crossing thresholds (smartphone cost, EV battery cost)  
- **Social**: behavioral/demographic shifts (urbanization, household size, generational change)  
- **Technology**: infrastructure breakthroughs (GPS on phones → delivery dispatching; 4G → short video)

**Heuristic from Marc Andreessen**: If you believe something will happen, try every 3 years.  
**Wang's rule**: If you haven't failed yet, entering earlier beats entering later — but survive the wait.

**Signal check**: Have predecessors tried and failed? Map their failure modes — was it wrong time, wrong method, or genuinely wrong need?

---

## Phase 9 — PMF & STP Analysis (产品市场匹配)

> "The sharper the entry wedge (切入点), the higher the early ROI."

### STP Framework

**Segmenting** — Choose the right coordinate system:
- Avoid too many axes (paralysis) or wrong axes (wrong conclusions)
- MECE principle: segments must be mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive
- Key dimensions often: income level, time availability, geography, age cohort, use occasion

**Targeting** — Select the entry wedge (NOT the final target market):
- Optimize for long-term strategic advantage of the wedge, not just current size
- Watch for "being carved up" risk: if your market is too broad, specialists will peel off each segment
- Consider organizational capability: can you actually serve this segment well today?

**Positioning** — Connect supply to demand in user's mind:
- What does your product *mean* to the target user?
- Can it be expressed in one crisp sentence / tagline?
- Test: would a user mis-categorize your product as an official/authoritative product? (Good sign —校内网 / Campus Network effect)

---

## Phase 10 — 4P Analysis (互联网时代的4P)

> "The most powerful internet business models compress one P to zero and monetize another."

Apply in this order: **Price → Product → Place → Promotion**

| P | Internet-era twist |
|---|--------------------|
| **Price** | Bits can be free; pricing determines demand segment. Who subsidizes whom (e.g., platform subsidy wars)? |
| **Product** | Fixed cost high, marginal cost near zero. What is the minimal viable version for the PMF wedge? |
| **Place** | Bits travel at light speed; no physical shelf. But attention = new shelf. What is the distribution channel? |
| **Promotion** | Zero-price products self-promote. Viral loops, PR, content seeding often beat paid ads. |

**Key pattern**: Look for opportunities to compress one P → 0 while monetizing a different P (360 killed antivirus price → became a distribution channel; Costco compressed promotion → monetized membership).

---

## Phase 11 — Supply-Demand Dynamics (供需关系)

> "Supply-demand determines everything in commercial products. Most teams analyze it wrong or ignore it entirely."

1. **Identify the scarce side**: Is supply or demand the bottleneck *right now*?  
2. **Space dimension**: Supply-demand balance varies by geography. Map hyperlocal vs. national vs. global.  
3. **Time dimension**: How fast does supply-demand cycle? (Ride-hailing: hourly; retail: seasonal; real estate: decadal)  
4. **Tiering**: High-end often has elastic demand (try-it buyers); low-end has inelastic demand (necessity). Which tier is supply-constrained vs. demand-constrained?  
5. **Non-market factors**: Regulation, labor law, infrastructure policy. These are low-frequency but high-impact.  
6. **Online vs. offline shift**: Internet often causes violent oversupply offline (your 3-block radius expands to city-wide), requiring business model restructuring.

---

## Phase 12 — Synthesis & Strategic Recommendation

Bring all lenses together:

1. **Market verdict**: How big, how fast, how concentrated will this be?  
2. **Scale effect verdict**: What type of curve, what scope? Who will win structurally?  
3. **Timing verdict**: Is the window open, about to open, or already closed?  
4. **Entry strategy**: What is the sharpest PMF wedge? What organizational capabilities are required?  
5. **Risks**: What are the 2–3 assumptions that, if wrong, would invalidate the entire thesis?  
6. **Watchlist signals**: What leading indicators should you monitor to update your view?

---

## Research Execution Notes

When conducting web research for this analysis:
- Search for **market data** (order volumes, revenue, user counts, growth rates)
- Search for **funding history** of key players (signals market conviction)
- Search for **regulatory news** (non-market factors)
- Search for **comparable international markets** (for analogy sizing)
- Prioritize **primary indicators** (actual usage, order data) over survey/self-reported data
- Be skeptical of market research reports that lack methodology — triangulate with multiple sources

Output should be structured as a research memo with clear section headers, key findings in plain language, and explicit uncertainty flags where data is thin.
